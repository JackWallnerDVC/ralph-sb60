Modern NFL defenses are built to stop the spread. They are lighter, faster, and designed to cover three or four wide receivers. This is exactly why San Francisco's reliance on "21 Personnel" (2 Running Backs, 1 Tight End, 2 Wide Receivers) is a mathematical nightmare for defensive coordinators.

## The Personnel Mismatch
When the offense puts a fullback on the field, the defense is forced to match with "Base" personnel (usually 4 defensive linemen and 3 linebackers) to respect the run threat. If they stay in "Nickel" (adding an extra defensive back), they risk getting mauled in the run game.

But here is the trap: San Francisco passes out of 21 Personnel more efficiently than most teams run out of it.

## The Kyle Juszczyk Factor
The key variable is the fullback's versatility. Because he can line up in the I-formation, motion to the slot, or split out wide, the defense cannot key on a specific tendency.

When the defense stays in Base (3 LBs), the fullback often draws a coverage matchup against a linebacker who lacks the lateral agility to cover a wheel route or an option route.

## SB60 Implication
Watch the defensive substitutions on the first drive. If the opponent matches 21 Personnel with Nickel (5 DBs), expect the 49ers to run the ball directly at the slot corner. If the opponent matches with Base (3 LBs), expect play-action passes targeting the seams.

The chess match isn't about the quarterback's arm; it's about forcing the defense into the wrong personnel grouping before the ball is even snapped.
